Arctic Mining Equipment and Technology: How Cold Changes Everything

By Margaret Olsen · 2026-03-23

arctic miningequipmenttechnologycold weather operations

Mining in the Arctic is not simply mining in a cold place. The extreme cold, permafrost, ice, and seasonal darkness fundamentally change how mines must be designed and operated. This article examines the technology and equipment considerations specific to Arctic mining.

Cold Weather Equipment Challenges

Hydraulic systems: Hydraulic fluids thicken at low temperatures, reducing equipment responsiveness and increasing wear. Standard hydraulic systems may fail at temperatures below -30°C. Arctic-specification equipment uses synthetic hydraulic fluids rated to -50°C or lower.

Electrical systems: Batteries lose capacity rapidly in cold temperatures. Lead-acid batteries may lose 50% or more of their capacity at -20°C. Lithium-ion batteries also suffer but to a lesser extent. This affects everything from vehicle starting to handheld equipment.

Steel and metals: Standard steel becomes brittle at low temperatures, increasing the risk of catastrophic failure. Arctic-grade steel with improved low-temperature toughness is required for structural components, mining equipment, and infrastructure.

Rubber and polymers: Rubber seals, tires, hoses, and polymer components become rigid and prone to cracking in extreme cold. Arctic-specification materials with improved low-temperature flexibility are essential.

Lubricants and fuels: Standard lubricants and diesel fuel may gel at Arctic temperatures. Arctic-grade diesel with cold-flow improvers and synthetic lubricants are required. Fuel heating systems may be necessary for extended cold periods.

Mining Equipment

Haul trucks: Large mining haul trucks (e.g., Caterpillar 797, Komatsu 930E) can be adapted for Arctic service with:

  • Engine block heaters and fuel heaters for cold starting
  • Arctic-grade hydraulic systems
  • Heated operator cabins with life-support systems
  • Insulated fuel and hydraulic tanks

    • Cost of Arctic-specification haul trucks is typically 15-25% higher than standard models.

    Excavators and loaders: Similar cold-weather adaptations are required for excavators, loaders, and dozers. Electric drive systems may be preferred over hydraulic systems for better cold-weather performance.

    Drilling equipment: Diamond drilling in permafrost requires specialized equipment:

  • Heated drilling fluid circulation to prevent freezing
  • Arctic-specification drill rods and casing
  • Heated mud mixing and pumping systems
  • Drill shelters with heating systems

    • Crushing and grinding: Processing equipment is less affected by cold (much of it operates indoors), but:

  • Ore handling and conveyor systems must handle frozen ore
  • Water-based processes require heating to prevent freezing
  • Lubrication systems must be rated for Arctic temperatures

    • Infrastructure Technology

    Power generation: Most Arctic mines rely on diesel generation:

  • Arctic-specification generators with fuel heating systems
  • Redundant power supply for critical facilities
  • Growing interest in wind-diesel hybrid systems for fuel savings and emissions reduction

    • Water treatment: Arctic water treatment systems must handle:

  • Freezing temperatures in outdoor components
  • Reduced biological treatment efficiency in cold water
  • Need for heated storage tanks and insulated piping

    • Buildings and structures: Arctic mining infrastructure must be designed for:

  • Extreme cold (insulation, heating, air barriers)
  • High winds (structural design)
  • Snow loads (roof design)
  • Permafrost (foundation design with thermosiphons or insulated foundations)

    • Communications and Navigation

    Communications: Remote Arctic locations rely on satellite communications:

  • VSAT satellite systems for data connectivity
  • Satellite phones for voice communication
  • Potential for Starlink or similar low-earth orbit satellite services

    • Navigation: GPS-based systems for vehicle tracking, fleet management, and survey:

  • Standard GPS works well at high latitudes (unlike near the poles)
  • Autonomous haulage systems are being tested in Arctic mining environments

    • Emerging Technologies

    Autonomous equipment: Autonomous haul trucks and drilling systems reduce the need for human operators in extreme cold, improving safety and productivity. Several mining companies are testing autonomous systems in Arctic conditions.

    Remote operations: Remote monitoring and control centers allow mining operations to be monitored and controlled from temperate locations, reducing the number of personnel required on-site.

    Battery-electric equipment: Electric mining equipment reduces diesel consumption and emissions. In Arctic conditions, battery performance in cold must be carefully managed, but indoor charging in heated facilities mitigates this challenge.

    Drones: Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are used for:

  • Aerial surveying and mapping
  • Stockpile volume measurement
  • Environmental monitoring
  • Infrastructure inspection

    • Cold-weather battery limitations affect drone flight time and must be accounted for.

    Maintenance in the Cold

    Equipment maintenance is more challenging and expensive in Arctic conditions:

  • Outdoor maintenance is limited by cold exposure for workers
  • Heated maintenance facilities are required for major equipment work
  • Parts supply chains are longer and more expensive due to remote locations
  • Cold-weather failures may be more frequent and harder to diagnose

    • Maintenance costs in Arctic operations are typically 20-30% higher than in temperate-zone operations.

    Conclusion

    Arctic mining requires specialized equipment, technology, and infrastructure that is fundamentally different from temperate-zone mining. Cold weather adaptations, permafrost engineering, and remote operations technology add cost but are essential for safe and productive Arctic mining operations. For Skaergaard, selecting the right Arctic-grade equipment and technology from the outset will be critical to project execution and long-term operational performance.
