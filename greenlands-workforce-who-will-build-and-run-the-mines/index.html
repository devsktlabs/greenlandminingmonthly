Greenland's Workforce: Who Will Build and Run the Mines?

By Margaret Olsen · 2026-03-23

greenlandworkforcelaboremployment

Greenland's total population is approximately 56,000 people, spread across the world's largest island. For mining companies planning large-scale operations like Skaergaard, the workforce question is central: who will build the mine, who will operate it, and where will they come from?

Greenland's Current Labor Pool

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Population: 56,000 (approximately)

  • Nuuk (capital): 19,000
  • Sisimiut: 5,600
  • Ilulissat: 4,600
  • Qaqortoq: 3,200
  • Other settlements: 23,600 (scattered across the coast)
  • East Greenland (including near Skaergaard): approximately 3,500

Employment: Greenland's economy is dominated by:

  • Public sector (government, education, health): ~40% of employment
  • Fishing and seafood: ~25% of employment
  • Tourism: ~10% of employment
  • Other (including some construction, services): ~25%

Skills base: Greenland has a relatively small pool of technically trained workers. The main educational institutions include the University of Greenland (Ilisimatusarfik) and several vocational schools. Mining-specific training programs are limited.

The Skills Gap

Greenland's mining workforce challenge is straightforward:

  • No existing mining workforce: Greenland has no large-scale mining operations (except the small ruby mine). There is no local pool of experienced mining engineers, metallurgists, heavy equipment operators, or mine planners
  • Small total population: Even if 100% of the working-age population were available, the pool is very small relative to mining labor requirements
  • Geographic distribution: Most of Greenland's population lives in West Greenland, far from Skaergaard in East Greenland
  • Competition with other industries: Fishing, tourism, and the public sector all compete for the same limited labor pool

Workforce Solutions

Mining companies operating in remote locations typically use several strategies:

FIFO (Fly-In, Fly-Out): The standard model for remote Arctic mining. Workers are flown to the mine site for rotations of 2-4 weeks, then flown home for 1-2 weeks of rest. FIFO allows access to a global labor pool while providing workers with regular home time.

Local hiring and training: Mining companies commit to hiring and training local workers, building skills in the community. This is both a social obligation and a business imperative (local workers are more likely to stay long-term).

International recruitment: Skilled mining professionals (engineers, geologists, managers) are typically recruited internationally, from mining jurisdictions like Canada, Australia, South Africa, and Scandinavia.

Seasonal labor: Some roles can be filled on a seasonal basis, aligning with the Arctic field season (June-September). This is most applicable to construction and exploration roles.

Skaergaard Workforce Plan

For Skaergaard specifically:

  • Construction phase: Peak workforce of 500-1,000, primarily international and Danish workers, with some Greenlandic hiring for support roles
  • Operations phase: Steady workforce of 300-500, with a target of 30-40% Greenlandic employment (growing over time as training programs develop)
  • East Greenland community: Engagement with Ittoqqortoormiit for employment, training, and business opportunities
  • Training programs: Pre-mine vocational training in mining operations, equipment operation, safety, and environmental management

Labor Costs

Arctic mining labor costs are higher than in established mining jurisdictions:

  • FIFO logistics (flights, accommodation, travel time) add 20-40% to base labor costs
  • Arctic premium compensation (hazard pay, remote location premium) adds 10-20%
  • Limited local labor availability increases reliance on expensive international workers

Total labor cost premium: approximately 30-60% above comparable temperate-zone mining operations.

Economic Impact

Mining employment can have a transformative economic impact on Greenland:

  • Direct employment: 300-500 high-paying jobs at Skaergaard
  • Indirect employment: Service, supply, and support jobs created in local communities
  • Skills development: Mining training programs develop transferable skills for the Greenlandic workforce
  • Revenue: Royalties, taxes, and corporate spending flow to the Greenland government
  • Diversification: Mining provides economic diversification beyond fishing and public sector employment

The Danish and Nordic Labor Connection

Greenland's relationship with Denmark and the Nordic countries provides a labor advantage:

  • Danish workers: Denmark has a skilled industrial workforce with mining and heavy industry experience (oil, wind energy)
  • Nordic mining expertise: Finland and Sweden have established mining industries with experienced workers and mining education programs
  • Cultural affinity: Greenlandic workers have cultural connections to the Nordic labor market
  • Regulatory alignment: Nordic labor standards and safety regulations are aligned, facilitating worker mobility

Comparison with Other Arctic Jurisdictions

Greenland's workforce challenge is significant but not unique:

  • Nunavut (Canada): Similar population dynamics (small, remote, indigenous communities). FIFO model used extensively. Inuit employment targets of 20-30% at established mines
  • Alaska (USA): Larger labor pool but still remote. Alaska Native hiring preferences exist at some operations
  • Svalbard (Norway): Small population, mining was historically important. International workforce used

Conclusion

Greenland's small population and lack of existing mining workforce represent a significant challenge for Skaergaard. The solution will be a combination of FIFO international workers, local hiring and training, and long-term workforce development. For investors, labor costs are a material component of Arctic mining economics, and the workforce plan is a key execution risk. The positive side is that mining employment offers transformative economic opportunity for Greenlandic communities and can build a skilled workforce that benefits the territory long-term.
