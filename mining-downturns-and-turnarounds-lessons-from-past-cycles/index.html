Mining Downturns and Turnarounds: Lessons from Past Cycles

By Margaret Olsen · 2026-03-23

miningcyclesdownturnturnaround

The mining industry is inherently cyclical. Prices rise, capital floods in, supply overtakes demand, prices collapse, capital retreats, and the cycle begins again. Understanding these cycles is critical for investors in development-stage projects like Skaergaard that must survive multiple market cycles between discovery and production.

Historical Mining Cycles

The 1970s boom: Commodity prices surged during inflation and geopolitical uncertainty. Mining investment increased significantly, leading to oversupply in the early 1980s.

The 1980s-1990s slump: Persistent oversupply and low prices caused widespread mine closures and industry consolidation. Many mining companies went bankrupt or were acquired at distressed prices.

The 2000s supercycle: Driven by China's industrialization, commodity prices reached unprecedented levels. Mining companies invested aggressively in expansion and new development.

The 2011-2015 bust: Oversupply from the supercycle investment hit the market just as Chinese demand growth slowed. Prices collapsed. Major mining companies wrote off hundreds of billions in asset value.

The 2020-2024 recovery: COVID-related supply disruptions, inflation, and geopolitical tensions drove commodity prices higher. Mining companies exercised capital discipline this time, avoiding the overinvestment of previous cycles.

2025-2026: A mixed environment. Gold remains strong due to central bank buying. PGMs face structural supply deficit but demand uncertainty from EV transition. Base metals are mixed.

How Companies Survive Downturns

Successful mining companies share common characteristics during downturns:

  • Low-cost position: Companies with lowest-quartile operating costs maintain margins even when prices fall
  • Strong balance sheet: Low debt and available liquidity provide a buffer against revenue decline
  • Quality assets: High-grade, long-life deposits retain value through cycles better than marginal assets
  • Capital discipline: Avoiding overinvestment during booms preserves optionality during busts
  • Multi-metal diversification: Projects producing multiple metals are less exposed to single-commodity price drops

Lessons for Skaergaard

Skaergaard's multi-metal profile provides natural downturn protection:

  • Gold as hedge: Gold tends to perform well during economic uncertainty and mining downturns. Skaergaard's 23.5M oz AuEq provides counter-cyclical revenue support
  • Palladium supply deficit: Even during palladium price weakness, the structural supply deficit (0.5-1.0M oz/yr) provides a floor under prices
  • Rhodium volatility: Rhodium's extreme price volatility is a risk, but its high unit value means even small production contributes meaningful revenue at current prices
  • Byproduct credits: Gold and rhodium byproduct credits reduce effective PGM costs, providing a buffer against palladium price weakness

Financing During Downturns

Development-stage projects like Skaergaard face additional challenges during downturns:

  • Equity markets: Capital for development-stage mining companies dries up during downturns. Equity financings become dilutive or unavailable
  • Project finance: Banks tighten lending standards during downturns. Debt financing may require higher equity contributions and shorter tenors
  • Government support: Counter-cyclical government support (grants, guarantees) can bridge the financing gap
  • Joint ventures: Strategic investors (offtakers, sovereign wealth funds) may provide financing in exchange for offtake agreements or equity stakes

The timing of financing relative to the commodity cycle significantly affects investment returns. Companies that finance during downturns and produce during upcycles generate the highest returns.

The Counter-Cyclical Investment Thesis

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Some of the best mining investments are made during downturns:

  • Lower entry valuations: Development-stage mining companies trade at significant discounts to intrinsic value during downturns
  • Construction cost advantages: Mining equipment and services are cheaper during downturns due to reduced demand
  • Strategic buyers: Major mining companies acquire distressed assets during downturns, providing M&A upside
  • First-mover advantage: Companies that advance projects during downturns are positioned to produce when prices recover

Risks of Counter-Cyclical Investing

  • Timing: Downturns can last longer than expected, consuming cash reserves
  • Survival risk: Some projects do not survive prolonged downturns
  • Political risk: Mining jurisdictions may change policies during prolonged downturns
  • Capital availability: Financing may not be available even at attractive project economics

Conclusion

Mining cycles are inevitable, and the current mixed environment requires careful navigation. Skaergaard's multi-metal profile provides natural downturn protection through gold byproduct credits and the structural palladium supply deficit. For investors, understanding where we are in the cycle and how Skaergaard's economics perform across price scenarios is essential. The counter-cyclical thesis is compelling for patient investors with long time horizons, but execution and financing timing are critical variables.
