The Greenlandic Inuit and Mining: Community Perspectives

By Margaret Olsen · 2026-03-23

greenlandinuitcommunityminingsocial license

Greenland's Indigenous Inuit majority has a complex relationship with mining development. For projects like Skaergaard, understanding and respecting community perspectives is not just ethical but essential for obtaining and maintaining the social license to operate.

Greenlandic Inuit and Resource Development

Greenland's population is approximately 88% Inuit (Kalaallit), making it one of the most Indigenous-majority jurisdictions in the world. The Inuit relationship with the land and sea is deeply cultural, spiritual, and practical:

  • **Hunting and fishing**: Subsistence hunting (marine mammals, fish, birds) remains an important part of Greenlandic culture and food security
  • **Cultural identity**: Inuit identity is closely tied to the Arctic environment and traditional practices
  • **Economic opportunity**: Many Greenlanders see mining as an opportunity for economic development, jobs, and modernization

    • Community Attitudes Toward Mining

    Greenlandic attitudes toward mining are nuanced and vary between individuals and communities:

    Support factors:

  • Economic opportunity: High-paying jobs, training, and career development
  • Infrastructure: Mining development brings roads, ports, airports, and utilities that benefit communities
  • Government revenue: Royalties and taxes fund public services and social programs
  • Modernization: Mining contributes to Greenland's economic diversification and self-sufficiency
  • National pride: Some Greenlanders view mining development as a step toward economic independence and eventual full sovereignty

    • Concern factors:

  • Environmental impact: Concerns about contamination of hunting grounds, marine environment, and freshwater
  • Cultural disruption: Fear that mining development could disrupt traditional lifestyles and cultural practices
  • Social change: Rapid economic development can bring social challenges (alcohol, housing, cost of living)
  • Distribution of benefits: Concern that mining benefits may not be equitably shared among communities
  • Loss of control: Some community members fear that mining companies and external interests will dominate decision-making

    • The Social License to Operate

    The social license to operate goes beyond legal permits. It requires:

  • **Meaningful consultation**: Genuine engagement with affected communities, not just information sessions
  • **Informed consent**: Communities must understand the potential benefits and risks before agreeing to a project
  • **Benefit sharing**: Clear mechanisms for distributing mining benefits to affected communities
  • **Grievance mechanisms**: Accessible processes for resolving disputes and concerns
  • **Ongoing relationship**: Maintaining community trust throughout the life of the mine and beyond

    • East Greenland Context

    The East Greenland communities near Skaergaard are small and remote:

  • **Ittoqqortoormiit (Scoresbysund)**: Population approximately 350-400. The largest settlement near Skaergaard. Residents depend on hunting and fishing, with limited economic alternatives
  • **Cultural significance**: The East Greenland coast is culturally important for Inuit communities, with deep connections to marine mammal migration routes and hunting grounds
  • **Infrastructure gap**: East Greenland has minimal infrastructure (no roads connecting communities, limited port and air facilities)
  • **Education and skills**: Limited local access to higher education and vocational training

    • For Skaergaard, building a constructive relationship with East Greenland communities is particularly important given the small population and limited alternative economic opportunities.

    Impact Benefit Agreements

    Impact Benefit Agreements (IBAs) are formal agreements between mining companies and affected communities that define:

  • Employment targets and training commitments
  • Local procurement preferences
  • Community infrastructure investments
  • Cultural and environmental protection measures
  • Revenue sharing or royalty arrangements
  • Monitoring and dispute resolution mechanisms

    • IBAs are standard practice in Arctic mining jurisdictions (Canada, Scandinavia) and are expected for Skaergaard.

    Lessons from Other Arctic Communities

    Other Arctic mining jurisdictions provide lessons for Greenland:

    Canada (Nunavut):

  • Inuit Impact Benefit Agreements are legally required for major projects
  • Inuit employment targets of 20-30% at operating mines
  • Inuit-owned companies provide mining services (catering, transport, security)
  • Challenges remain: high turnover, housing shortages, social impacts

    • Sweden (Sami communities):

  • Mining on traditional Sami reindeer grazing lands has been contentious
  • Legal disputes over land rights and cultural protection
  • Growing emphasis on FPIC (Free, Prior, and Informed Consent)

    • Alaska (Iñupiat communities):

  • Alaska Native corporations own surface and subsurface rights in some areas
  • Revenue sharing through royalty arrangements
  • Alaska Native hire preferences at some operations

    • Community Engagement Best Practices

    Effective community engagement for Arctic mining includes:

  • **Early engagement**: Start engaging communities during the exploration stage, not just during permitting
  • **Cultural respect**: Engage community elders and traditional knowledge holders alongside political leaders
  • **Transparency**: Provide clear, honest information about project plans, risks, and benefits
  • **Employment pipeline**: Establish training programs well before construction begins
  • **Local business development**: Create opportunities for local businesses to participate in the supply chain
  • **Long-term commitment**: Demonstrate commitment to the community beyond the life of the mine (infrastructure, skills)

    • Conclusion

    Greenlandic Inuit communities are the ultimate arbiters of mining's social license in Greenland. Their support is essential for project success, and their concerns must be taken seriously. For Skaergaard, building a constructive, respectful relationship with East Greenland communities is both a moral imperative and a practical business requirement. The mining industry's track record in other Arctic jurisdictions provides both positive examples and cautionary tales. Getting community engagement right from the outset is one of the most important things GRML can do for Skaergaard's long-term success.
